Introduction
Morgan Stanley Investment Management (“MSIM”)1 is the investment
management division of Morgan Stanley2 and provides broad-based
specialisation across a range of asset classes in public and private markets.3
MSIM has been creating value for its clients for more than 45 years and
operates in more than 20 countries. MSIM has $1.9 trillion in assets under
management (“AUM”) as of 31 December 2025. MSIM’s independent
investment teams leverage Morgan Stanley’s global resources to serve a
diverse client base of governments, institutions, corporations, advisers and
individuals worldwide. MSIM’s investment solutions include a broad range
of alternatives, high-conviction active strategies, customisation strategies,
sustainability expertise and tax management solutions.
MSIM believes that sustainability factors can present investment risks and opportunities
which, when properly understood and managed, allow our investment teams to both
mitigate risk and generate long-term investment returns. Engagement and stewardship are a
key part of this understanding. Investment teams may engage with the assets or companies
they own, seeking to deliver long-term value. This approach aligns with our objective to be
responsible stewards of our clients’ capital.
To support the delivery of tailored, value-added investment solutions to clients, MSIM
takes a decentralised approach towards investment management, consisting of independent
public- and private-market investment teams and asset class platforms. This decentralised
investment approach allows investment teams to be responsible for tailoring and developing
their individual approaches to sustainable investing, consistent with their fiduciary duty,
using multiple factors including, but not limited to, the objectives of the product, asset class
and investment time horizon, geographic considerations, as well as the specific research and
portfolio construction, philosophy and process used by each team. Investment teams assess

Scenario Narratives
NET ZERO 2050
The Net Zero 2050 narrative (“orderly transition”) considers that increased international collaboration and technological
advancement results in the globe being on track to limit global warming to 1.5 degrees Celsius.
In this scenario, global total CO2 emissions continue to decline, hitting net zero by 2050. Global temperature rise is limited to 1.5°C
by 2100 through immediate climate action with global coordination across policymakers. The policy includes a global harmonised
carbon price, and a thriving sustainable finance market fuels significant investment in clean energy. Notably, developments in artificial
intelligence and other technologies lead to higher power demands but support innovations in clean tech and energy efficiency. Costs
falls for decarbonisation solutions such as energy storage and carbon capture. Sectors such as transport and power experience
significant decarbonisation. Real estate construction booms as buildings are renovated or replaced with more climate-resilient and
efficient tech. Consumers also help to accelerate the transition with higher demand for low-carbon products.
FRAGMENTED WORLD
The Fragmented World narrative (“disorderly transition”) concerns the global fragmentation of financial markets due to global
success against net zero targets being hampered by division and ulterior motives.
In this scenario, global total CO2 emissions are reduced gradually, but net zero targets are not achieved by 2050. Global temperature
rise is limited to 2.3°C by 2100 through fragmented climate action lacking coordination globally. Global total CO2 emissions are taxed
only starting in 2030, enabled through a surge in carbon prices that differ by region. Deferred climate policies lead to higher physical
risk, especially in the tropical regions, while also incorporating transition risk from a disorderly sudden transition. Despite growing
need in power demand for artificial intelligence and other technologies, alternative energy sources fall short, and there is a lack of
technological breakthroughs for decarbonisation solutions in the early years. While there are some ambitious and meaningful climate
efforts, global success is ultimately hampered by division and misalignment between countries.
HOT HOUSE WORLD
The Hot House World narrative (“hot house world”) concerns growth results in a shift away from curtailing emissions with climate
regulations being relaxed, leading to significant physical and economic impacts due to climate change.
In this scenario, policymakers take insufficient climate action to reduce global total CO2 emissions. Instead, they prioritise economic
growth, and global temperature rise climbs to 3.5°C by 2100. Global CO2 emissions are not taxed, and there is no further policy action
for climate mitigation. Despite growing power demand for artificial intelligence and other technologies, alternative energy sources fall
short, and there is a lack of technological breakthroughs for decarbonisation solutions in the early years with the gap filled with coal
and gas. Critical temperature thresholds are exceeded, leading to severe physical risks and irreversible impacts.
