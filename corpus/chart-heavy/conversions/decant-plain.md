# CONTENTS

CERN, the European Organization for Nuclear Research, is the world’s leading
laboratory for particle physics. It provides a unique range of particle-accelerator
facilities enabling research at the forefront of human knowledge. Its business
is fundamental physics, finding out what the Universe is made of and how

it works.

Founded in 1954, CERN has 25 Member States, with other nations from around
the globe also contributing to and participating in its research programmes.
The Laboratory has become a prime example of international collaboration,
uniting people from all over the world in the quest to push back the frontiers of
science and technology for the benefit of all.

### MESSAGE FROM THE PRESIDENT OF THE COUNCIL

Particle physics is at a critical point, as decisions on the
successor to the Large Hadron Collider must be made soon
to ensure continuity, capability and leadership. Preparing
for CERN’s future was thus the overriding theme of Council
business during 2025.

The Council’s approval of the CERN Medium-Term Plan for
2026–2030 secured the necessary resources for the safe
and timely completion of the High-Luminosity LHC upgrade,
the Phase 2 upgrades of ATLAS and CMS and the work
scheduled for Long Shutdown 3. Additional resources were
allocated to consolidating the North Area, upgrading ISOLDE
and extending the operation of MEDICIS, thereby ensuring
that CERN continues to provide a broad community with a
compelling scientific programme. The Council also approved
the use of isotopes produced at MEDICIS for clinical
treatment, reinforcing CERN's commitment to translating
scientific research into societal benefits.

The Council appointed the CERN senior leadership team
for 2026–2030, as proposed by the Director-General
designate, Professor Mark Thomson. It welcomed Slovenia
as a Member State and Ireland as an Associate Member
State and approved the admission of Chile as an Associate
Member State, subject to completion of the necessary
ratification process.

Turning to the future of CERN, major progress was made
towards the ongoing 2026 update of the European Strategy
for Particle Physics (ESPP), which called on the particle
physics community to develop a visionary and concrete plan
that greatly advances human knowledge in fundamental
physics through the realisation of the next flagship project
at CERN. The Council was pleased to see the very large
number of contributions from all areas of high-energy
physics received by the deadline of 31 March.

Key input was the Future Circular Collider (FCC) Feasibility
Study, which was mandated by the Council in response to
the recommendation of the 2020 ESPP update. The Study
was completed ahead of schedule and was summarised in a
three-volume report that was released in March.

The Council-appointed European Strategy Group (ESG)
and the Physics Preparatory Group reviewed the ESPP
contributions, and the full spectrum of community input was
discussed at a highly successful Open Symposium held in
Venice in June. An overwhelming majority of the communities
from the CERN Member States expressed their strong
support for the FCC, based on its superb physics potential

and its long-term prospects for exploring the energy
frontier. As requested by the Council, they also discussed
alternative options if the FCC turns out not to be feasible
or competitive.

Following reviews by the Council’s subordinate bodies and
by independent expert committees, the Council examined
the FCC Feasibility Study report at a dedicated meeting in
November 2025. Delegates reaffirmed their strong wish to
maintain CERN as a leader in particle physics and viewed the
continuation of the FCC study as a step towards this goal.

The drafting session of the 2026 ESPP update took place
in December 2025, and revealed a clear consensus that the
FCC is the highest priority project. At its December session,
the Council thanked the ESG for its outstanding work and
took note of its recommendations, with a view to updating
the Strategy in May 2026 at a dedicated Session to be held
in Budapest.

On behalf of the CERN Council, I would like to thank CERN
and the high energy physics (HEP) community for having
participated very actively in the 2026 ESPP update. It is
rewarding to see that the scientific opinion of the HEP
community is in sync with the 2020 update, the decision of
the CERN Council to initiate the FCC Feasibility Study and
the efforts of the CERN Management to steer and complete
it. This is a sign of the strength of the community.

Costas Fountas

### 25 BY ’25: CONCLUDING CERN’S DIVERSITY TARGET INITIATIVE

Launched in 2021 and inspired by the European
Strategy for Particle Physics, the 25 by ’25 initiative
was CERN’s first Organization-wide diversity target.
It set two goals: to increase the proportion of women
among staff and postgraduates to 25% by the end of
2025, with a particular focus on STEM roles, and to
strengthen nationality diversity in line with Member
State contributions.

In December 2025, when the initiative concluded,
women represented 24.7% of the employed staff and
postgraduate population, compared with 21% at the
start. The actions that were most successful included

the appointment of departmental Diversity & Inclusion
Officers, the introduction of offboarding surveys to
understand any diversity or inclusion-related retention
issues and the creation of recruitment dashboards
to track nationality and gender diversity in the
recruitment process.

Building on these results, a new initiative called
Inclusion Matters has been proposed for 2026 onwards
to consolidate progress and reinforce long-term
attraction and retention by embedding inclusion in
everyday practices.

# EXPLORING THE NATURE OF THE UNIVERSE

To investigate the fundamental structure of the Universe, CERN operates a
unique complex of accelerators that collide particle beams head on or direct
them onto fixed targets. The products of these collisions are recorded by giant
detectors and studied by thousands of scientists at the Laboratory and across

the globe.

### CERN’S ACCELERATOR COMPLEX AND THE EXPERIMENTS THAT IT SUPPLIES

The Large Hadron Collider (LHC) is CERN’s flagship machine,
colliding beams of protons and other particles at the highest
energies ever achieved. The products of these collisions are
recorded by ALICE, ATLAS, CMS, LHCb and other, smaller
LHC experiments. 2025 was the final full year of LHC Run
3 and saw an exceptional volume of data collected at a
collision energy of 13.6 teraelectronvolts (TeV). This milestone
was complemented by the release of many first-class
scientific results by experiments at the LHC and at other
accelerators at the Laboratory. For the LHC experiments,
these included the first results from the first collisions

between light nuclei at the LHC, observations by CMS
and ATLAS of a subtle feature of the behaviour of
top-quark pairs produced at the minimum possible energy,
suggesting that top quarks can momentarily pair up with
their antimatter counterparts, and LHCb’s first observation
of the phenomenon of CP violation in decays of three-quark
particles called baryons. Among the results from the non-LHC
experiments, BASE announced the first demonstration of an
antimatter quantum bit and NA61/SHINE reported that a type
of symmetry between up and down quarks is more broken
than expected.

### THE HIGGS BOSON UNDER THE LENS

Since the discovery of the Higgs boson at the LHC in 2012,
the ATLAS and CMS Collaborations have been measuring
this unique particle with ever increasing precision.

In 2025, to shed light on how the Higgs boson interacts with
itself, ATLAS significantly enhanced the statistical power of
its analysis of one of the “golden” decay channels of a pair
of Higgs bosons, where one Higgs boson decays into two
photons and the other into a pair of bottom quarks. The
result marked the first ATLAS measurement based on over
300 inverse femtobarns of proton–proton collision data. In
a step towards understanding the interaction between the
Higgs boson and “second-generation” matter particles,
ATLAS found evidence of the decay of the Higgs boson into
two muons with a significance of 3.4 standard deviations,
confirming a previous CMS result.

Meanwhile, CMS released its full combination of Run 2
measurements across all accessible Higgs-boson production
modes and decay channels, representing one of the most
comprehensive characterisations of the Higgs boson to date.
CMS reported the results of the first search for a Higgs boson
decaying into a pair of charm quarks in collision events where
the boson is produced alongside two top quarks. This novel
search, which exploited cutting-edge AI techniques, was
used to set the most stringent limits to date on the interaction
between the Higgs boson and the charm quark. The results
represent an improvement of around 35% compared to
previous constraints and place significant bounds on
potential deviations from the Standard Model prediction.

### TESTING THE STANDARD MODEL AND ITS EXTENSIONS

The LHC Collaborations continue to subject the Standard
Model and its possible extensions to stringent tests.
The Standard Model allows for an asymmetry between
matter and antimatter that is known as charge–parity
(CP) violation. But, perplexingly, this asymmetry is many
orders of magnitude too small to account for the matter–
antimatter asymmetry observed in the Universe. In 2025,
the LHCb collaboration reported the first observation of CP
violation in decays of three-quark particles called baryons.
The discovery provides new ways to explore the matter–
antimatter imbalance in the Universe and to address why the
elementary particles that make up matter fall into the neat
patterns described by the Standard Model.

Another LHCb highlight was the first dedicated measurement
of the mass of the Z boson at the LHC. The measurement
resulted in a mass of 91 184.2 MeV with an uncertainty of
just 9.5 MeV – or about a hundredth of a percent. The result
demonstrates the level of precision that can be achieved
at the LHC despite the complex environment of proton–
proton collisions, in which many particles are produced
simultaneously. It also opens the door for future studies of
the Z boson mass at the LHC, as well as at the upcoming
High-Luminosity LHC and proposed future colliders.

LHCb also released the results of a new analysis of angular
observables associated with the rare decay of the beauty

confirm a tension with predictions from the Standard

2025 saw CMS and ATLAS report the observation of a
subtle feature of the behaviour of pairs of top quarks (the
heaviest and shortest-lived of all the elementary particles)
produced at the minimum possible energy. First reported
by CMS and then confirmed by ATLAS, the result suggests
that top quarks can momentarily pair up with their antimatter
counterparts to produce a “quasi-bound state” called
toponium. Further input based on complex theoretical
calculations of the strong nuclear force will enable physicists
to understand the true nature of this elusive state.

ATLAS presented the first evidence of vector boson
scattering – in which two W or Z bosons scatter off one
another – involving “longitudinally polarised”, same-sign
W bosons. If such a boson is in a longitudinally polarised
state, its intrinsic spin is oriented perpendicular to its
direction of motion. By precisely measuring this polarisation,
physicists can perform powerful tests of the Standard Model.

In tests of the internal consistency of the Standard Model,
ATLAS reported a new measurement of the mass of the top
quark using LHC Run 2 collision events with high-transverse-
momentum top quarks. The new result, 172.95 ± 0.53 GeV,
is ATLAS’s most precise top-quark mass measurement using
a single decay channel and is in excellent agreement with
the combination of measurements from ATLAS and CMS
using LHC Run 1 data.

ATLAS used its full LHC Run 2 dataset to confirm the
presence of the all-charm tetraquark X(6900). Composed
entirely of heavy charm quarks, such exotic four-quark
particles provide an extreme yet more theoretically tractable

playground for understanding the strong force than their
lighter equivalents.

Meanwhile, CMS took an important step in disentangling
the nature of these hadrons with the first measurement
of the quantum properties of a family of three all-charm
tetraquarks that comprises X(6600), X(6900) and X(7100).
The measurements indicated that these exotic hadrons are
more likely to be made of tightly bound quarks.

CMS reported observing for the first time the production
of a single top quark along with a W and a Z boson using
an advanced machine-learning algorithm. The study of
this extremely rare process, which occurs only once every
trillion proton collisions, could point researchers to signs of
phenomena beyond the Standard Model.

In further tests of the Standard Model, CMS continued its
comprehensive study of vector-boson scattering, which
culminated in the observation of this fundamental process
for all six possible pairs of heavy vector bosons.

### SMASHING LIGHT IONS AND MORE

Colliding ions with one another allows physicists to study
quark–gluon plasma (QGP), an extreme state of matter
that mimics the conditions of the Universe during its first
microseconds, before atoms formed. Until now, exploration
at the LHC of this hot and dense state of matter relied on
collisions between heavy ions, such as lead and xenon,
which maximise the size of the plasma droplet created.
Collisions between lighter ions, such as oxygen, open a
new window to better understand the characteristics and
evolution of QGP. Not only are these ions smaller than lead
and xenon, allowing better investigation of the minimum size
of nuclei needed to create QGP, but they are less regular
in shape. A neon nucleus, for example, is predicted to be
elongated like a bowling pin, although it is vastly smaller in
comparison.

In 2025, the LHC Collaborations presented the first results
from the first oxygen–oxygen and neon–neon collisions at
the LHC (p. 27). The experiments focused on measurements
of subtle “flow” patterns in the angles and directions of the
particles flying outwards as the QGP droplet expands and
cools, which are caused by small distortions in the original
collision zone. ALICE, which specialises in the study of QGP,

### ANTIMATTER ARENA

CERN’s Antimatter Factory provides low-energy antiprotons
to experiments that study the properties and behaviour of
antimatter and compare them with those of normal matter.
These comparisons test a fundamental symmetry of the
Standard Model called charge-parity-time invariance and a
principle of general relativity known as the weak equivalence
principle.

In 2025, low-energy antiprotons from the ELENA ring, which
slows down antiprotons even further than the facility’s
Antiproton Decelerator, were routinely delivered to the
AEgIS, ALPHA, ASACUSA, BASE and GBAR experiments.
Meanwhile, advances were made in the development of
the PUMA and BASE-STEP experiments, which aim to
transport antimatter to other facilities for nuclear-physics and
antimatter studies, respectively.

The ALPHA Collaboration reported a new technique to
increase the rate of production of antihydrogen atoms
eightfold. The technique involves a novel way of cooling
positrons before they are merged with antiprotons to form
antihydrogen atoms, allowing over 15 000 antihydrogen
atoms to be accumulated in under seven hours. Before
this, it took several weeks to produce a similar quantity
of antihydrogen.

In 2025, the BASE Collaboration reported that it had been
able to keep an antiproton oscillating smoothly between two
different quantum states for almost a minute while trapped.
The feat, which was possible thanks to a substantial upgrade
of the experiment, marked the first demonstration of an
antimatter quantum bit and paves the way for measurements
of the magnetic moment of the antiproton with 10- to
100-fold improved precision.

Another achievement was the development by the AEgIS
Collaboration of a new detector that uses modified mobile
phone camera sensors to record in real time the points
where antimatter annihilates with matter. The device can
pinpoint antiproton annihilations with a resolution of about
0.6 micrometres, representing a 35-fold improvement over
previous real-time methods and significantly enhancing
AEgIS’s ability to study the effect of gravity on antihydrogen
in the future.

### SCIENTIFIC COMPUTING

In 2025, scientific computing activities for the LHC
experiments remained focused on supporting an intensive
data-taking programme. The four large experiments
recorded an exceptional volume of data, collecting 252 PB
during the year, representing a 15% increase compared to
2024. These datasets were transferred from the experimental
sites to the CERN data centre in Meyrin for storage,
long-term archival and processing. Subsequently, they
were transferred to the data centre in Prévessin (Tier-0)
and, from there, to the 148 sites across more than
40 countries of the Worldwide LHC Computing Grid (WLCG)
for further processing, end-user analysis and long-term
custodial storage.

The overall data volume produced by the LHC experiments
has surpassed the level of 1 exabyte (EB) now stored
on CERN-managed storage and made accessible to the
worldwide scientific community. This milestone reflects not
only the remarkable physics output of the experiments,
but also the sustained scalability and reliability of the
underlying storage and data management infrastructure.
Efficient hardware lifecycle management, combined
with performance optimisation, ensured that this rapidly
expanding data volume remained at all times accessible
by the large scientific community.

Operationally, WLCG delivered another year of stable and
efficient service. All pledged compute and storage resources
from participating sites were delivered on schedule and
were fully utilised to support core workflows, including
data reconstruction, large-scale Monte Carlo production
campaigns and user-driven analysis activities.

Global data movement performance remained a key strength
of the distributed infrastructure. Average sustained transfer

rates exceeded 70 GB/s throughout the year. The system
demonstrated particular robustness during dedicated heavy-
ion runs, which generated a large volume of data throughput
to the Tier-0. Despite the demanding high load, no incidents
were reported during the run. This achievement underscores
the resilience of operational procedures, the effectiveness of
monitoring and alerting systems and the strong coordination
between computing centres worldwide. In November 2025,
for several hours, the IT teams performed a stress test of
the software that manages the processing activities
(HTCondor), in preparation for the High-Luminosity LHC
(HiLumi LHC).To do this, they sent a huge number of tasks to
be processed in an attempt to crash the system. The system
held up well under such heavy-stress conditions, and the
resilience it demonstrated confirms the infrastructure’s
readiness for further scaling in preparation for HiLumi LHC
data production.

Beyond day-to-day operations, 2025 was marked by
progress in technical solutions and long-term planning.
The WLCG Technical Coordination Board moved forward
with the drafting of a multi-year roadmap, in collaboration
with experiments and sites, which defines priorities for
service evolution, infrastructure modernisation, software
optimisation and architectural changes required for the
HiLumi era.

To validate readiness and guide future improvements,
several “Data Challenges” were organised during the year.
These exercises tested data transfer performance at scale,
evaluated new middleware components and stressed
distributed storage systems under realistic production-like
conditions. In parallel, the transition from X.509 certificate-

progressed steadily.

### THE HIGH-LUMINOSITY LHC

The High-Luminosity LHC (HiLumi LHC), a major upgrade of
the LHC, will increase the integrated luminosity by a factor
of ten compared with the original LHC machine. The project
involves the use of innovative technologies that are also of
great interest for future accelerators and for applications
beyond high-energy physics. 2025 saw major advances in
the series production of most of the components, ready for
the start of installation in the LHC tunnel during the third
long shutdown (LS3) (p. 25).

A crucial feature of the upgrade is the installation of
innovative superconducting focusing magnets on either
side of the ATLAS and CMS experiments. These magnets
are made of a superconducting material called niobium–
tin (Nb3Sn), which will allow higher magnetic fields to be
achieved. Two types of these magnets will be installed:
a short version (4.2 metres long), produced by the US
collaboration AUP (Accelerator Upgrade Programme), and
a longer version (7.2 metres long), produced at CERN. Half
of the short versions have been delivered by the FNAL
Laboratory. By the end of 2025, over 80% of the magnet
cold masses required for the HiLumi LHC upgrade, including

the long versions, had been produced and validated at cold.
The CERN teams reached the production pace required to
complete the cryostating activities. The first novel beam
screen featuring tungsten shielding and an integrated beam
position monitor was inserted into a cold mass of one of
the long magnets. This achievement, which is the final
step in the cryostating of a cold mass before the magnet’s
installation, marks an important milestone.

The magnets are powered by an innovative cold-powering
system. All of the components (magnesium diboride cables,
REBCO tape, cryostats) have been delivered to CERN
ready for assembly. The magnets are protected during
quenches by a new system known as CLIQ. All of the 24
CLIQ protection units have been delivered to CERN and the
validation testing is under way.

The crab cavities will guide the particle bunches in such
a way as to increase the size of the beam intersection
area. Two types of crab cavity are being built: the double-
quarter wave (DQW) cavities, which are being produced
in Europe, and the radiofrequency dipole (RFD) cavities,
which are being produced in the US as part of the AUP
collaboration and in Canada. All of the DQW cavities have
been manufactured in industry and validated at cold at
CERN. Four fully equipped cavities have been sent to the
STFC Daresbury Laboratory in the United Kingdom to allow
it to start assembling the first two DQW cryomodules. The
first series RFD crab cavity was successfully tested at cold
at the Jefferson Laboratory in the United States, then sent
to the TRIUMF Laboratory in Canada, where the first RFD
cryomodule will be assembled.

The detailed design of the beam dumps has been validated.
The dumps are made up of isostatic graphite sections and
central sections consisting of reinforced carbon capable of
withstanding the most extreme thermal shock conditions.
The procurement of the raw materials has begun, and
prototyping activities have allowed the production steps
to be validated. The pre-series collimators, which are
another component of the machine protection system, were
delivered in December and will be tested in 2026.

### THE FUTURE CIRCULAR COLLIDER STUDY

The Future Circular Collider (FCC) Study concerns a
91–kilometre circular collider, with eight surface sites
around its circumference, and paves the way for an
ambitious physics programme. The FCC Collaboration,
which comprises 162 institutes in 38 countries, completed
the three-volume Feasibility Study report and additional
supporting documents in March 2025. The Feasibility Study
provided essential input for the update of the European
Strategy for Particle Physics. The report was submitted to
the various bodies of the CERN Council, a process that
concluded in November with a special Council session. The
Council recognised that the FCC would be a platform for a
visionary physics programme addressing many of the open
questions in particle physics. It concluded that the Feasibility
Study provided the basis for the FCC studies to continue
and that the funding scenarios and the financial pledges
obtained so far were essential milestones on the path to
potential project approval.

The involvement of local communities is an important
component of the project. Several hundred people attended
public meetings and discussions that were organised in
France and Switzerland to present the progress of the Study,
including the geotechnical investigations. In the autumn, a
series of meetings in the municipalities where the FCC sites
would be located provided an opportunity for more than
400 people to talk directly to the Study teams.

On the technical front, 2025 was marked by a transition
from validation of the design to integrated system-level
demonstration for the FCC-ee. The FCC design is based
on two superposed rings: a booster accelerates particles
to varying energies (ranging from 45.6 GeV to 182.5 GeV),
depending on the running mode, before injecting them
into the collider. Superconducting radiofrequency cavities
accelerate the electrons and positrons in the booster and
compensate for synchrotron radiation losses in the collider.
Significant progress was made in the development of the
cavities (p. 45).

Magnet studies also progressed well, with the consolidation
of specifications and the preparation of a roadmap towards
prototyping and industrialisation. A short prototype of a low-
field dipole conductor magnet for the booster was built and
tested, validating the design.

The teams continued to work on a full-scale mock-up of an
arc half-cell, the most recurrent section of the accelerator.
The purpose of this mock-up is to provide a platform for
integration, alignment, stability and installation studies in
realistic conditions. Xsuite is a simulation platform for the
accelerator lattice design, beam dynamics, interaction
between particles and matter and single-particle tracking.
This multifunctional toolkit underwent a series of significant
upgrades, including the addition of spin-tracking capabilities
and the expansion of lattice design features.

### MORE EFFICIENT ACCELERATING CAVITIES

### LINEAR COLLIDERS

The development work for the FCC centres on superconducting
cavities and radiofrequency (RF) amplifiers that are more
efficient and consume less energy. This work is part of the
SRF (Superconducting Radiofrequency) R&D programme
being conducted by twelve institutes from five countries.

In 2025, the reverse phase operation scheme, which involves
using the same RF configuration for the Z, the WW and the
ZH boson production energies, was successfully validated.

The collider’s 400 MHz cavities are made of copper coated
with a niobium film on their inner walls. Scaled 1.3 Ghz
cavities achieved the required specifications. Several
activities are under way to optimise the manufacturing and
surface preparation processes in order to reduce the internal
defects that impair performance. Bulk-machining tests
were carried out on cavities with a simplified geometry. This
process avoids welding on the cavities’ central axis, but is
costly when it comes to mass production. An alternative
hydroforming technique is currently being evaluated,
and various welding and internal coating techniques are
being studied. A new optical inspection system has been
developed to detect defects on the inner surface.
For the booster’s 800 MHz cavities, which are made
of bulk niobium, two simplified prototype cavities were
manufactured. These prototypes will be used to select the
surface treatments that are the most effective at reducing
losses and improving overall performance.

The development of the power couplers has been adapted
to alternating phase operation, and a 400 MHz prototype
resonance ring has been designed for use in the qualification
testing. The use of tristons, a type of klystron that offer
excellent operational flexibility along with efficiency gains,
has been validated. Demonstrators are being developed
at CERN for the 400 MHz models and by a collaboration
involving SLAC and Thales for the 800 MHz models.

The finalisation of the RF reference parameters, the
advancement of tunnel integration studies and the design of
the cryomodules were further important milestones in 2025.

CLIC (Compact Linear Collider) is a linear high-luminosity
electron-positron collider proposed in three energy stages,
ranging from 380 GeV to 3 TeV. In 2025, the Collaboration
published a comprehensive report as input for the update
of the European Strategy for Particle Physics. Among other
things, the report describes the X-band technology on which
the CLIC concept is based, the technical design of the
collider – with updated parameters and optimised luminosity
for all the energy levels – and revised cost and energy
consumption estimates. CLIC’s innovative acceleration
technology was evaluated for use in neutron and synchrotron
light sources. At the same time, work on the International
Linear Collider (ILC) continued as part of European efforts
coordinated via the ITN (ILC Technology Network). Studies
carried out by CERN, KEK (Japan) and European institutes
covered key technical areas. Work on the siting, civil
engineering and costing for an ILC-type facility at CERN
progressed and the results were part of the input provided
for the update of the European strategy.

### HIGH-FIELD MAGNETS

The High-Field Magnet (HFM) Programme is an international
collaboration of 12 institutes with the aim of developing
accelerator magnets that generate operational magnetic fields
of 14 tesla (T). Two avenues are being explored, one based on
low-temperature superconductors, mainly niobium-tin (Nb3Sn),
and another on high-temperature superconductors that would
allow magnetic fields of 20 T to be reached.

Magnets made of Nb3Sn have reached maturity thanks to
the HiLumi LHC. Institutes are using different approaches to
develop demonstration and short-model magnets. At CERN,
the racetrack model magnet (RMM) made from flat coils was
reassembled and tested, demonstrating the reproducibility of
the results. A field of 16.95 T was measured in a 50-millimetre
diameter aperture. The teams began developing a dipole
magnet made from Nb3Sn coils in a block configuration, as well
as the associated manufacturing tools. Fabrication is planned
for 2026.

INFN is working on a classic cosθ set-up: a structure with
dummy coils was built to validate the mechanical design.
CERN, PSI and CIEMAT are also exploring the possibility of
coils with a combination of niobium-tin and niobium-titanium
in the areas where a weaker magnetic field is sufficient. This
option would reduce costs and limit hysteresis losses during
the ramp-up of the magnetic field.

The second research avenue is magnets using high-
temperature superconductors, namely REBCO (rare-earth,
barium copper oxide) materials. Conclusive tests were carried
out on the REBCO tape production line at the Karlsruhe Institute

of Technology (KIT) using pulsed laser deposition technology.
A dipole demonstration magnet was assembled and tested at
CERN in a common coil set-up with a 20-millimetre aperture.
This small demonstrator generated a field of 5.6 T. The CEA
team developed 60-centimetre flat coils, also using REBCO
(this time with metal insulation), which reached a field of 5.2 T.

### A NOVEL PROJECT TO EXPLORE THE HIDDEN SECTOR

The purpose of the HI-ECN3 (High-Intensity Experimental
Cavern North 3) project is to install a beam dump facility
(BDF) in the ECN3 underground hall for the SHiP (Search
for Hidden Particles) experiment, which is scheduled
to start operation in 2031. ShiP is designed to explore
the hidden sector by detecting very weakly interacting
particles, which might include candidate dark-matter
particles.

The BDF includes a high-density target designed to absorb
a high-intensity, high-energy proton beam from the SPS.
A pure tungsten target cooled by gaseous helium was
chosen as the baseline solution. Two prototypes composed
of different tungsten blocks were tested with beam in 2025.
A third prototype, equipped with a helium cooling station,
was developed, a major milestone for the project and a
world first in the field, and will be tested in 2026. Around
618 tonnes of cast iron blocks were excavated from an
old neutrino facility at the PS, then cleaned and set aside
to serve as shielding for the BDF. This reuse of materials
from old facilities is part of the sustainable development
approach adopted for the project.

Studies conducted as part of the North Area consolidation
project, aimed at upgrading the primary beamlines,
have made significant progress. They relate to the full
refurbishment of the TCC2 hall, the modernisation of the
beamlines and target systems and the installation of a new
beam collimation system in order to reduce beam losses
and background for SHiP.

As part of the DECRYCE project, a prototype using curved
silicon crystals was developed to reduce beam losses
during the slow extraction of protons from the SPS. The
prototype was successfully tested on a test line and was
then installed in the SPS, ready for testing in situ in 2026.

The SHiP Collaboration, which brings together 45
institutes from 18 countries, has made significant progress
with respect to the design of the detector, the preparation
of its commissioning and the definition of the experiment’s
physics goals. The muon shielding system, consisting of
several magnets, notably includes the magnetic hadron
stopper inside the BDF. Detailed studies and a new
machine-learning system made it possible to simulate
several hundred configurations, reduce the length of the
hadron stopper and define two options for the muon shield
magnets. Technical solutions for integrating the neutrino
and light dark matter scattering detector (SND) into this
shielding have also been identified.

A tank filled with gaseous helium was selected as the
decay volume, obviating the need for a complex metal
vacuum vessel that would have produced significant
background noise. This tank’s membrane was thoroughly
tested, allowing the performance requirements of the
detector that surrounds the decay volume and identifies
the background, known as the Surrounding Background
Tagger (SBT), to be lowered. Beam tests were performed
on the aluminium and the scintillator liquid of this
detector’s cells.

Progress was also made in the design of the spectrometer,
which consists of a straw-tube tracker, a time-of-flight
detector and a magnet. Prototyping of the straws
continued and a second magnet demonstrator was
built. This dipole magnet is made of superconducting
magnesium diboride wires so as to considerably reduce
power consumption compared to a normal-conducting
electromagnet.

### WAKEFIELD ACCELERATION

The AWAKE experiment tests the acceleration of electrons
using a plasma wakefield generated by proton beams. This
technology would make it possible to reach acceleration
gradients hundreds of times higher than those that can
be achieved with traditional cavities. Following initial tests
that validated the concept, a second series of tests began
in 2023 using a ten-metre-long rubidium vapour plasma
source divided into segments whose temperature can be
controlled. The first phase of this second series of tests,
which was completed in June, demonstrated that electron
energy can be increased by varying the density of the
plasma along the source. These tests also helped to refine
the configuration of the next phase of the experiment, which
will use two plasma sources and will be commissioned after
LS3. Significant progress has been made in the design and
prototyping of the electron source and the components of
the future beamline and the preparations for manufacture.
The collaboration also pursued its efforts to develop scalable
plasma sources. The dismantling of the experiment began in

June, making AWAKE the first facility to enter the third long
technical shutdown (LS3) (p. 25).

### LHC EXPERIMENTS UPGRADE

Operation of the HiLumi LHC will dramatically increase the
number of simultaneous collisions that take place inside
the ATLAS and CMS detectors, from around 60 to between
140 and 200 per bunch crossing. To be able to efficiently
sort and record the vastly increased amount of data, both
collaborations are undertaking ambitious upgrades of their
detectors. ALICE and LHCb are likewise preparing upgrade
programmes for key subdetectors to ensure that they
remain at the forefront of precision measurements and rare-
event detection.

Upgrades to CMS include the endcap High-Granularity
Calorimeter (HGCAL), which features a high-granularity
electromagnetic section based on hexagonal silicon sensors
segmented into small hexagonal cells. The hadronic section
uses the same technology, combined with a less segmented
scintillator-tile section in regions of lower occupancy and
radiation. The HGCAL absorber arrived at CERN from
Pakistan, and pre-series cassette assembly and testing are
ongoing at both Fermilab and CERN.

The CMS Outer Tracker (OT) and Inner Tracker (IT) detectors
will feature increased forward acceptance, granularity
and radiation tolerance. The OT will also provide tracking
information to the Level-1 Trigger. Production of the IT
sensors is now complete.

Some of the electronics for the barrel calorimeters will
be replaced to accommodate the increased data flow
expected from the HiLumi LHC. Production of all front-end
electronic components has begun, as has testing of the
electromagnetic calorimeter safety system. A full system
integration test took place on an SPS beamline in October.

support structures for both pixels and strips began in
the second half of 2025. The initial production yield was
determined and the production rate was subsequently
ramped up. These detector upgrades will be supported
by new data acquisition and trigger systems. Validation of
the design of key custom components is concluding, with
production due to start in 2026.

Subtle issues were found in a common electronics
component that is used by both ATLAS and CMS for
multiple upgrade projects, and mitigation measures are
being considered.

Preparations for LHCb Phase 2 (Upgrade II) are under way.
While the full Upgrade II is scheduled for the HiLumi LHC
era, critical “LS3 enhancement” components will be installed
or prepared during LS3. The ring-imaging Cherenkov (RICH)
system is undergoing a massive electronics overhaul to be
able to handle increased occupancy. Following successful
prototype testing, final production of the FastRICH chips
is under way. These will provide the precise time stamping
of photons that is needed to identify particles in a high-
density environment. The electromagnetic calorimeter is
being adapted to withstand the high radiation levels of the
HiLumi LHC with improved spatial and temporal resolution.
It has a “spaghetti calorimeter” design, which incorporates
tungsten absorbers and radiation-hard crystal fibres, and has
moved from the prototyping phase to the construction of the
innermost modules.

ALICE is preparing to upgrade its inner tracking system
(ITS3), which uses bent silicon pixel sensors located closer
to the beamline than the present tracker. ITS3 will therefore
significantly enhance the heavy-flavour and di-electron
programmes of the experiment. The complete engineering
model has been built, and the full sensor production process
is under way. The Forward Calorimeter is a combined
electromagnetic and hadronic calorimeter optimised to
measure photons close to the beamline. Its electromagnetic
section is a highly granular silicon–tungsten system, while the
hadronic section is a copper–scintillator sampling system.
The detector design has been finalised and procurement of
components and sensors has begun. Good progress has
been made in silicon sensor R&D and detector design for
ALICE 3, which is proposed for HiLumi LHC Run 5. This
will provide considerably improved tracking precision and
extended acceptance coverage.
